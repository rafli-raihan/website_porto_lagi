Releasing Your First Music in the Big '26 (as a WNI, SEUMUR HIDUP)

7 min read

2024-06-26

Zero Capital, High Spirits

Here I am, a songwriter and bassist in a band called amongthehow. In the big 2026, we set out on a quest that's borderline delusional but quite fun and epic, producing and releasing our music into the wild, independently. No labels, minimal capital, and a production skill level that started near absolute zero. Oh, and did I mention we are doing this as legal Indonesian citizens 4 life (WNI, seumur hidup).

You've heard it many times across the internet of how easy and doable this is, but the here's the thing, most of them were said by someone with at least enough capital or skill to put out a decent production right from where they started. Now you're gonna hear (or read) this from a complete beginner point of view, someone who's actually learning it as they go. So yeah, welcome to the world of indie music... today.

The Good

If you have a creative itch, the modern digital landscape is like a massive playground where you can easily turn limitations into your own advantage. You don't even need to own a physical instrument anymore, I write my songs using mobile apps like BandLab and n-tracks by mapping out the entire melodies and arrangement via MIDI grids. If you have an audio interface and at least a couple of instruments lying around, tracking at home in a makeshift studio is entirely viable, thanks to the existence of free DAWs like Cakewalk and Reaper (this one's actually not, it's like the WinRaR of DAWs), along with good free VSTs we have nowadays like NAM (Neural Amp Modeler) for example, giving you absolute flexibility over your pacing and deadlines without a commercial studio clock burning through your wallet. For the uninitiated, the internet serves as a massive peer-to-peer academy filled with endless YouTube tutorials, forums, communities and yes even AI (not gen-AI obviously) ready to help you learn tracking, mixing, and mastering from scratch.

When it comes to distribution, independent platforms have completely opened up global delivery for everyone. We chose CDBaby's basic tier, paying a one-time fee of roughly Rp 190,000 for a lifetime release under an 80/20 royalty split. They handle the boring technical setup across international streaming platforms. Reaching a global audience from your bedroom is fully achievable if you write your lyrics in English and learn to decode how international social media meta works.

The Bad

Of course, stripping away the corporate label machine means you inherit all the logistical friction, and the independent path is romantic until the invoices start rolling in. If your home gear lacks the necessary quality, you are forced to rent commercial tracking spaces, and securing a decent small-to-medium recording studio in Jakarta will quickly drain your budget. Furthermore, video tutorials and text message consultations can only take a beginner so far. Ultimately, our band hit a creative wall and had to bring in someone with the expertise to audit, fix, and elevate our debut track through the complex mixing and mastering phases where we were completely stuck.

The "budget-friendly" illusion breaks down quickly when you calculate the aggregate expenses. Even with our ultra-lean, cost-cutting approach, our basic single cost us roughly Rp 800,000 to manufacture and launch—broken down into Rp 500,000 for professional mixing/mastering, Rp 190,000 for publishing, and around Rp 100,000 to Rp 120,000 for post-release traction so far.

To make matters tougher, the modern indie musician is forced to live a double life as a full-time content creator. You aren't just competing on the sonic quality of your chords, you are fighting for attention against a staggering tidal wave of thousands of new songs uploaded to streaming platforms every single day. You can optimize your upload schedules, manipulate search tags, and study the trends, but true visibility frequently behaves like pure luck. Combine that with the reality that navigating music royalties and copyright enforcement in Indonesia remains a notoriously chaotic, bureaucratic maze, and you realize the odds are heavily stacked against you.

The Ugly

Let's drop the romanticized rockstar filter for a moment, high expectations in this game equals to emotional disappointment. Really... from a cold, transactional economic standpoint, the ratio of input effort to financial return makes absolutely no sense. The barrier to entry has never been lower, yet the probability of commercial viability remains microscopic especially in a society where art is treated as a strict tertiary luxury (WNI, SEUMUR HIDUP). Spending your hard-earned cash on a release feels less like building a financial asset and more like buying a disposable toy or food, you pay for it, you consume and listen to it, and the money is gone with zero immediate financial return.

The fairy-tale era of talent scouts discovering raw street kids and turning them into cultural icons is officially dead. The shared culture has shattered, there is no universal medium like the 80s MTV broadcasts or the 90s Grunge explosion to unite the masses. Music is hyper-fragmented into isolated digital pockets, meaning you have to keep your ego firmly checked. You must constantly ask yourself, who are you in an ocean containing millions of other creators screaming for the exact same second of attention?

Why We Do It Anyway?

If the math is terrible and the odds are stacked against us, why are we still pressing "Upload"? Because we shifted our perspective from financial speculation to personal growth. At the end of the day, our band wanted to learn. The only investment that offers guaranteed returns is knowledge, and by pushing through this first release cycle, me and the band gained an immense amount of practical insight, at least enough for me to write this blog. When you reframe your debut releases as an alternative tuition fee for upskilling in audio engineering, digital marketing, and content creation, the monetary cost suddenly looks incredibly cheap.

There is also an irreplaceable sense of personal ownership in building a portfolio. Seeing a creative concept that spent months stuck as a basic MIDI file inside a mobile phone finally transition into a verified track streaming on Spotify and YouTube is an unreal sensation. For someone who spent their entire life looking at the music ecosystem strictly from the audience's point of view, stepping into the shoes of a verified artist is a massive personal victory. And of course, doing it all together as a band. It's the kind of struggle that we'd look back upon in the coming years, who knows? It could be a start of something big, or we may never make it anywhere in music. But without a doubt, this is gonna be a story that I'll cherish forever (getting sentimental are we?).

Ultimately, making music and pushing it out into the world today is relatively easier, but it's also completely uncertain. If you can check off even a fraction of the baseline technical requirements or minimal equipment, stop overthinking and launch the project. If you are still hesitant, aggressively reach out to peers, ask questions, and audit the exact tools you currently have at your disposal before deciding to commit your resources. Treat it like the ultimate creative sandbox, enjoy the process, and let the music play out.
